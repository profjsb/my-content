Bloom's SciPy 2012 keynote argues that Python has become the "super glue" and de facto engine of modern science, walking through how his UC Berkeley group uses it across the entire astronomy workflow: Bayesian astrometry with SciPy, quasar identification from light curves, roboticizing the PAIRITEL infrared telescope (with a live demo of Python 2.3.3 code still running it after eight years), and machine-learned real/bogus classification in the Palomar Transient Factory that enabled the discovery of SN 2011fe (PTF11kly), the nearest Type Ia supernova in three decades. He frames the coming LSST data deluge (~20 TB/night, a million supernovae a year) as demanding fully automated discovery, classification, and follow-up with humans out of the loop. He closes with Python education efforts at Berkeley (bootcamps, the IPython notebook as a teaching tool) and a call for open-source parallelism and R-competitive native ML/statistics packages in Python.

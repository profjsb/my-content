Josh Bloom (UC Berkeley) surveys how machine learning has become central to time-domain astrophysics, from real/bogus classification of transient detections (enabling the earliest-ever discovery of a nearby Type Ia supernova) to an ML-filtered search for Planet 9 on NERSC supercomputers. He describes moving beyond hand-engineered features to self-supervised autoencoders for irregularly sampled variable-star light curves and U-Net denoising of cosmic rays in Hubble images. The talk's central theme is "physics-informed ML": baking physical knowledge into architectures (e.g., periodicity-aware convolutions in polar coordinates), latent spaces (physics-conditioned VAEs for generating realistic light curves), and loss functions, plus emerging likelihood-free inference methods for recovering physical parameters from data. Presented at the Matroid ScaledML 2020 conference.

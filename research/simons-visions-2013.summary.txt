In this 2013 Simons Institute talk, Bloom traces the "modern astrophysics stack" from Galileo's notebooks and the Harvard women computers to today's fully automated pipelines, arguing that LSST-scale data (billion-source light curves, ~20 TB/night by ~2020) forces humans out of the real-time loop of observing, discovery, classification, and follow-up. He describes his roboticized telescope network (with a telescope event language and even a "starbucks" resource currency), the Palomar Transient Factory's real/bogus machine learning classifier that discovered the nearest Type Ia supernova in 30 years just 11 hours post-explosion, probabilistic variable-star catalogs across ~25 classes, and active/transfer learning across surveys. He closes by proposing "machine intelligence" (ML put into practice on noisy, dirty, real-world data) over academic toy problems, and asks whether machines can ever replace human eureka moments like Henrietta Swan Leavitt's period-luminosity discovery.

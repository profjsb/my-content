In this ~27-minute talk to the MMDS (Modern Massive Data Sets) audience, Josh Bloom argues that astronomy's data deluge — from LSST's projected billion light curves updated every three days to Gaia and 21-cm radio surveys — makes it impossible for humans to inspect data directly, demanding machine learning and automated inference in service of physical science. He describes his group's work building featurization pipelines and classifiers (with taxonomy-aware loss functions and active learning) that turn noisy, irregularly sampled light curves into probabilistic catalogs of variable stars across ~25 classes, enabling both high-purity demographic studies and novelty discovery of rare fading stars. He also presents a real-time "real-bogus" discovery engine using random forests within the Palomar Transient Factory pipeline, which enabled the earliest-ever observation of a supernova (~11-12 hours after explosion) via fully robotic discovery and follow-up. He closes by urging methodologists to work on questions domain scientists actually care about, and highlights the newly founded Berkeley Institute for Data Science (BIDS) as an incubator for such collaborations.

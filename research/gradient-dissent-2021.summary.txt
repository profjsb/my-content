In this Gradient Dissent podcast episode with Lukas Biewald, Josh Bloom (Chair of UC Berkeley Astronomy) discusses why astronomers so often move into machine learning, framing astronomy as a physics laboratory with big open questions: dark matter and dark energy, tests of general relativity via gravitational waves, and habitable exoplanet atmospheres. He recounts his ML origin story building a random-forest "real/bogus" detector for transient discovery, argues astronomy has not yet had its "fire the linguists" NLP-style ML moment — ML remains an accelerant and surrogate rather than a driver of new insight — and highlights likelihood-free inference and uncertainty quantification as key frontiers. Practical threads include his 2002 Python roboticization of a mothballed telescope, Berkeley's Python/Jupyter culture, the SkyPortal React/Tornado/Postgres stack, and the difficulty of putting ML into production in science, where "you always get an answer" without guardrails.

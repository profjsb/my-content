In this ~30-minute CITRIS Computational Science and Engineering lecture at UC Berkeley, Josh Bloom surveys the astrophysics of streaming time-series data: the dynamic universe of variable stars and explosive transients (supernovae, neutron-star mergers as future gravitational-wave sources) and the coming data deluge from surveys like LSST (~200 PB, 800M sources updated every 3 days) and Gaia. He describes his Center for Time Domain Informatics' work on the Palomar Transient Factory, where expert-labeled "real vs. bogus" training data and machine learning removed humans from the discovery loop, compressing ~10^5 nightly candidates down to the ~10 genuine new transients worth scarce follow-up resources. He details featurization of noisy, irregularly sampled light curves and taxonomy-aware classification of variable stars (reaching ~95% efficiency at 98% purity for transient identification and ~5% misclassification for broad variable classes), and argues that autonomous classification is abstracting the traditional role of the astronomer, with real-time alerts (via his VOEvent protocol) even enabling citizen-science follow-up.

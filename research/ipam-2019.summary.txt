Summary: Joshua Bloom: "Physics-Informed (and -informative) Generative Modelling in Astronomy" (IPAM, Machine Learning for Physics and the Physics of Learning 2019)

Josh Bloom (UC Berkeley) surveys how astronomers use machine learning, from bread-and-butter supervised classification (real/bogus vetting in the Palomar Transient Factory that enabled the 11-hour discovery of the nearest Type Ia supernova in decades) to self- and semi-supervised learning with autoencoders on irregularly sampled, uncertainty-weighted variable-star light curves, plus a U-Net for cosmic-ray detection and inpainting in Hubble images. He then describes physics-informed generative modeling: a conditional VAE/TCN that injects physical parameters (e.g. effective temperature) into the latent space to simulate realistic variable-star light curves for LSST cadence planning, alongside GAN-generated weak-lensing maps and surrogate models for expensive supernova and gravitational-waveform simulations. He closes with speculative ideas on generative catalog compression (e.g. shrinking the 1.2 TB Gaia catalog to megabytes while preserving aggregate science) and onboard autoencoder-based compressed sensing to beat downlink bandwidth limits on space telescopes like JWST.

In this ~2.5-hour Astro Hack Week 2016 lecture at the Berkeley Institute for Data Science, Josh Bloom gives a hands-on introduction to machine learning for astronomers, framing ML as one tool among many along three axes: Bayesian vs. frequentist, theory-driven vs. data-driven, and in-core vs. out-of-core computation. Working through a Jupyter notebook with SDSS data, he covers supervised regression (predicting quasar redshifts from photometry with linear models, k-nearest neighbors, and random forests) and classification (a star/galaxy/quasar separator), emphasizing feature engineering with domain knowledge, train/test splits, cross-validation, hyperparameter optimization (grid search, joblib, Dask), confusion matrices, out-of-bag error, and feature importance. He argues random forests are usually the best practical first choice over SVMs (which lack probabilities and require metric assumptions), discusses deep learning and autoencoders (including where they failed for the PTF real-bogus problem), and stresses that accuracy is not always the right metric and that ML rarely handles measurement uncertainty. He closes with his group's Cesium time-series ML platform, probabilistic variable-star catalogs (ASAS/bigmacc.info), predicting stellar parameters from Stripe 82 variability, and cautions about hidden biases and implicit priors in probabilistic catalogs.

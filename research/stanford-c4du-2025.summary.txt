In this ~20-minute Stanford Data Science faculty talk on time-domain data and anomaly detection, Bloom argues that the point of anomaly detection in astronomy is not methodology papers but demonstrating genuinely new physics, which requires multimodal, multi-wavelength follow-up. He shows how his group's contrastive multimodal foundation models (light curves, spectra, metadata in a shared 512-dimensional embedding) naturally cluster variable-star classes, split Mira variables into known subclasses unsupervised, and surface mislabeled objects and in-class outliers — but also repeatedly flag "amazing" anomalies that turn out to be bad data (a bad spectral reduction, aperture-contaminated photometry mimicking a 250-day Cepheid), underscoring the curse of dimensionality and the need for domain experts in the loop. He advocates moving anomaly detection upstream onto telescope telemetry and raw images, adding physical gut checks and inductive bias to representations, and looks ahead to agentic LLM "Occam's razor" workflows that rank anomalies against the literature and eventually propose and test physical models autonomously.

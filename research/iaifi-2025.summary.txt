In this 2025 IAIFI (MIT) colloquium, timed around Rubin Observatory/LSST first light, Bloom surveys AI across the full astronomy data lifecycle: midstream (real/bogus discovery, the Oracle hierarchical classifier, SN 2011fe follow-up), self-supervised representation learning and symmetry-padded "polar" convolutions for label-starved variable-star classification, and multimodal CLIP-style foundation models combining light curves, spectra, and metadata that dramatically improve low-label classification and surface mislabeled objects, unseen classes, and in-class anomalies via embedding-space searches. He recounts how neural posterior estimation (simulation-based inference, ~10^5x faster than MCMC) for exoplanet microlensing led his student to discover a previously unknown unifying degeneracy in the gravitational lens equation, later proven mathematically. Downstream, he describes LLM summarization and recommendation engines in SkyPortal; upstream, RL with physics-based reward functions for kilonova follow-up and a neural wavefront-estimation system for Rubin's active optics about to be deployed on-sky, closing with a call to put AI into production and to build foundation models others can actually use.

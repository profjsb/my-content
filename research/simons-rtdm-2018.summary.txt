Josh Bloom (UC Berkeley Astronomy) surveys the state of machine learning for time-domain astrophysics at the Simons Institute, motivated by the 2017 neutron-star merger and the coming LSST/ZTF alert fire hose, arguing that cheap discovery paired with expensive follow-up demands automated real-time discovery and inference. He covers real/bogus classification with random forests in the PTF pipeline (which enabled the earliest-ever detection of a nearby Type Ia supernova), real-time source-type classification, gamma-ray burst redshift ranking from Swift telemetry despite tiny label sets, and probabilistic variable-star catalogs built from ~75 handcrafted features. He then presents an autoencoding recurrent neural network that learns light-curve features in an unsupervised way, handling irregular sampling and measurement uncertainty, and matching or beating handcrafted-feature classifiers. He closes with open challenges: novelty detection, transfer learning across surveys, using networks as physics emulators, cadence optimization, and the sociological problem of globally coordinating telescope follow-up resources.

Josh Bloom (UC Berkeley astronomy professor and department chair), introduced by Pavlos Protopapas, gives an hour-long Harvard IACS seminar on machine learning in time-domain astrophysics. He covers ML in production for transient discovery (real/bogus classification, the early detection of a nearby Type Ia supernova, and a 10-millisecond-latency ML filter for the Planet Nine shift-and-stack search at NERSC), and self-/semi-supervised learning: recurrent autoencoders for irregularly sampled variable-star light curves and a U-Net that masks and inpaints cosmic rays in Hubble images (which spontaneously learned a Laplace-transform kernel). The core theme is physics-informed ML — phase-invariant "cyclic-padded" temporal convolutional networks (ITCNs) for periodic stars, physics-conditioned variational autoencoders that generate realistic light curves as a function of temperature and other stellar parameters, and likelihood-free inference for binary microlensing posteriors ahead of the Roman Observatory. Extended Q&A covers Bayesian nonparametrics vs deep learning, out-of-distribution detection, concept drift, and the dangers of unvetted ML in production.

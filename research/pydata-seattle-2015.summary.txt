In this ~46-minute PyData Seattle 2015 keynote, Josh Bloom presents machine learning systems as "living systems" of imperfect parts with weak contracts, taking Google's "ML: the high-interest credit card of technical debt" paper (5% ML code, 95% glue code) as his point of departure. Working outward from algorithms to software, hardware, people, consumers, and society, he argues that real-world ML must optimize across many axes — accuracy vs. memory/CPU/time-to-implement, explainability for non-technical stakeholders, and loss functions tied to actual use cases (ICU alerts vs. drone targeting; the never-deployed Netflix Prize winner) — rather than chasing benchmark metrics like AUC. He describes Wise.io's internal tooling (WiseDataSet, WiseTransfer, and the "wind tunnel" statistical regression-testing framework for accuracy, memory, and timing), concept drift and feedback-loop pitfalls in deployed models, and his NSF-funded open platform for ML on scientific time-series data (with Docker-based CI on Azure). He closes with fault-tolerant ML (Gmail/Netflix mistakes), the iris-dataset critique of asking questions no one cares about, and a call for interdisciplinary "gamma-shaped" teams — data science as a team sport.
